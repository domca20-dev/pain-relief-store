## 2B. Language Patterns (Tone, Structure, Common Rants/Confessions)

### Pattern 1: Absolutist Language
They speak in absolutes — "always," "never," "nothing," "everything," "every day," "all the time." There are no partial experiences. Pain is total, solutions are total failures, and despair is total.
- Example: "I've tried EVERYTHING available to me with NO luck."
- Example: "There is NOTHING that helps."
- Example: "The pain is 24/7. There is NO break."
- **Copy implication:** Mirror their absolutes. Don't soften. "Nothing has worked" is more resonant than "many things haven't worked."

### Pattern 2: Before/After Framing
Almost every post contains an unprompted comparison to their former self. They structure their identity as two people: who they were before pain, and who they are now.
- Example: "I used to be the person who organized everything. Now I can barely organize my pills."
- Example: "I went from running marathons to struggling to walk to the bathroom."
- Example: "In my brain, I long to be the person I used to be."
- **Copy implication:** The before/after contrast IS the hook. You don't need to invent dramatic tension — they carry it naturally.

### Pattern 3: Confessional Tone
Posts read like late-night diary entries. ALL CAPS emotional peaks. Run-on sentences. Unfiltered. Often written between midnight and 3am (they mention specific times).
- Example: "PAIN CHANGES PEOPLE!"
- Example: "I am becoming very sick and tired of being accused of being a drug addict. PISSED OFF!"
- Example: "Sometimes I think it would be easier to just not exist."
- **Copy implication:** Match the raw tone. Short paragraphs. Emotional peaks. Conversational, not polished. The ad should feel like a 2am Facebook post, not a brochure.

### Pattern 4: Lists of Failed Solutions
They catalogue their attempts compulsively — almost as proof that they've "earned" the right to complain.
- Example: "I've tried physical therapy, cortisone shots, heating pads, TENS, acupuncture, chiropractic, and six different medications."
- Example: "The drawer full of products I bought at 2am."
- Example: "$800 a month on treatments that weren't working."
- **Copy implication:** In the "Debunk" section of your ad, list 3-4 things they've tried and explain WHY each one failed (because of the UMP — surface-level treatment). This validates their experience and opens them to a new mechanism.

### Pattern 5: Desperate Questions
Their questions are cries for help disguised as inquiries. They're not looking for information — they're looking for someone to understand.
- Example: "Does anyone else feel like they're mourning the life they should have had?"
- Example: "Any of you feel so damn useless having chronic pain?"
- Example: "Has anyone found ANYTHING that actually works long-term?"
- **Copy implication:** These questions make perfect hooks. Use them verbatim as opening lines: "Has anyone found anything that actually works?" Then answer: "I thought the same thing. Until..."

### Pattern 6: Physical Degradation Imagery
They describe their body as a machine breaking down — mechanical language that depersonalizes the experience as a coping mechanism.
- Example: "I creep upward to a standing position."
- Example: "My body is betraying me."
- Example: "It feels like someone drove a hot spike through my spine."
- Example: "My knees sound like bubble wrap."
- **Copy implication:** Use their mechanical/visceral imagery in the "Vulnerability & Stakes" section. Physical descriptions are more hook-worthy than abstract emotional claims.

## 2F. Mass Desires (5 Distinct "I Want" Statements)

### Desire #1: "I want to get through a full day without pain dictating what I can and can't do."
- **Who:** Daily sufferers aged 45-70 whose entire routine is organized around pain

- **Intensity × Scope:** ★★★★★ × MASS — Broadest desire. Expect most CBO spend.
- **Awareness:** Problem Aware
- **Ad entry:** Open with the morning routine — alarm, dread, 90-second process of getting out of bed.

### Desire #2: "I want to be present for my family instead of watching life happen from the sidelines."
- **Who:** Grandparents 55-75, parents 40-60 missing birthdays, recitals, holidays, everyday moments
- **Core Need:** Family connection. Being the grandparent/parent they imagined being.
- **Intensity × Scope:** ★★★★★ × BROAD — Strongest emotional trigger. May win on conversion rate.
- **Awareness:** Problem Aware → Unaware
- **Ad entry:** Open with specific family moment — grandchild running, school play, holiday dinner.

### Desire #3: "I want to stop being a burden and do basic things for myself again."
- **Who:** People 50-70 dependent on spouse, adult child, or caregiver for dishes, driving, dressing, showering
- **Core Need:** Self-reliance. Dignity. Not being "taken care of."
- **Intensity × Scope:** ★★★★ × BROAD — Shame-driven. Powerful but needs empathetic copy.
- **Awareness:** Problem Aware
- **Ad entry:** The small indignity — needing help to bathroom, spouse carrying groceries, watching them take over your role.

### Desire #4: "I want to stop relying on pills that fog my brain and make me feel like an addict."
- **Who:** Adults 40-70 on daily prescription pain meds (Tramadol, Gabapentin, Lyrica, opioids)
- **Core Need:** Chemical independence. Control over their mind without pharmaceutical reliance.
- **Intensity × Scope:** ★★★★ × MODERATE-BROAD — Narrower audience but high intent. May win on ROAS.
- **Awareness:** Solution Aware
- **Ad entry:** The pill bottle moment — counting tablets, pharmacist's look, brain fog at 2pm.
- **⚠️ COMPLIANCE:** Never tell people to stop medication. Position as complementary. "Talk to your doctor."

### Desire #5: "I want to feel like the person I was before pain took over my life."
- **Who:** People 45-65 who once defined themselves by energy, productivity, social life, or physical capability

- **Awareness:** Unaware → Problem Aware
- **Ad entry:** "You used to be the person who..." then pivot: "And now you..." The contrast IS the hook.
